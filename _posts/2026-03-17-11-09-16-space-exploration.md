## The Next Century of Space: From Goddard's Spark to a Multi-Planetary Future

A century ago, Robert Goddard's liquid-fueled rocket soared for a mere 2 seconds, proving rockets could reach space and setting the stage for humanity's grandest adventures. Mocked in his time, Goddard's vision is now the bedrock of an accelerating era of space exploration, marked by ambitious returns, groundbreaking science, and a burgeoning commercial frontier.

Today, as we commemorate that pivotal launch, humanity stands poised to return to the Moon. NASA's Artemis program, with its sights set on a crewed lunar mission as early as April, signifies more than just a revisit. With centers like Huntsville at the helm, this endeavor is about establishing a sustainable presence, a stepping stone for deeper journeys. The "coming age of space stations" is not just theoretical; we're seeing commercial ventures like Axiom Station actively building next-generation platforms, signaling a vibrant, multi-stakeholder future for orbital outposts. Even national governments, like Canada with its $200M investment in a Nova Scotia launch pad, are bolstering the infrastructure vital for this expanding cosmic economy.

But our gaze extends far beyond the lunar surface. Scientific missions continue to unravel the universe's mysteries, from NASA's twin spacecraft deciphering Mars’ lost atmosphere to astronomers discovering new exoplanet types that "smell like rotten eggs." The tantalizing prospect of life thriving on moons of starless "rogue planets" pushes the boundaries of astrobiology, a field consistently advanced through research like the NASA Spaceline list. Protecting our home planet also remains crucial, with initiatives like Blue Origin’s ambition to defend Earth against dangerous asteroids underscoring the expanding scope of our space-based endeavors.

Central to this progress is the human element. Astronaut health is paramount, with innovations like "tissue chips in space" paving the way for advanced biomedical research in microgravity. And behind every launch, every discovery, are dedicated individuals like Jennifer Vaughn, contributing to organizations like The Planetary Society, pushing the boundaries of what's possible.

From Goddard's initial spark to a future where commercial stations orbit Earth, Artemis missions plant flags on the Moon, and probes explore distant worlds, the next century promises an exponential leap in our understanding and utilization of space. We are not just exploring; we are beginning to inhabit.
